Citizen App and the Controversy of User-generated Content

Shuang Wang, Dec 8th,2024  

User-generated content (UGC) has revolutionized how communities access and share information, offering unprecedented opportunities for real-time engagement and empowerment. Platforms like Citizen exemplify this transformation, enabling ordinary individuals to become active participants in public safety. By blending crowdsourced content with official data, Citizen reshapes narratives, fosters transparency, and bridges information gaps in underserved areas. However, this innovation also comes with significant challenges. From misinformation to over-surveillance, the risks of UGC highlight the complexities of balancing technological advancement with ethical and social responsibility. 

UGC Unveiled: Citizen’s Gateway to Real-Time Safety and Community Engagement 

User-generated content (UGC) is a vital feature of Citizen. By combining UGC with information from official sources, Citizen has built a platform that facilitates real-time sharing of local emergency events, empowering community members to actively participate in the dissemination of safety information. Citizen’s UGC features encompass a wide range of functionalities. Users can post their own incident reports, upload photos and videos, and comment on ongoing events. If a user is near the event, they are encouraged to “Go Live” and stream video to the platform which can be viewed by others in real time. This highly interactive model transforms users from passive recipients of information into active creators and sharers of critical content.

Citizen’s UGC feature stands out in its ability to deliver timely and transparent information during critical events. For example, during the 2017 pipe bomb attack near Times Square, Citizen demonstrated its capacity to disseminate vital updates at remarkable speed. Within just three minutes of the explosion, notifications had reached 627 users within a 5-mile radius. By the 12-minute mark, live streams from the scene were being viewed by over 7,000 users within a 1-mile radius. The reactions on social media were overwhelmingly positive and thankful. With no question, these alerts reached the individuals that were most likely to be in danger far faster than traditional news reporting could have. 
To ensure the credibility and reliability of the information shared, Citizen claims that every reported incident is reviewed by their team in real time and it is confirmed by scanning police radio before being made public. This process is crucial in preventing the spread of misinformation, especially during sensitive events such as false alerts about terrorist attacks, which could cause undue panic. However, despite its undeniable potential, UGC also brings with it a host of challenges and controversies. Issues such as the accuracy of UGC, user behavior, and the broader societal implications of crowdsourced reporting highlight the complexities of this approach. 

Using Crowdsourcing for a Safer Society: When the Crowd Rules 

The essence of UGC is a form of crowd-sourced information. It represents a type of online participative activity where volunteers contribute their diverse knowledge and experiences in response to an open call by an organization. As a crime strategist noted when discussing Citizen, “Rather than having a small, elite force of highly trained government agents here to protect us all, we’re much better off having average and ordinary citizens approaching this problem as a group and seeing what we can do.” In these scenarios, the crowd, which is sometimes seen as a threat, becomes an invaluable resource that can complement traditional law enforcement through collective intelligence. 
To favor participation in such initiatives, the reward offered is usually a key element. It is necessary to establish effective incentive mechanisms to attract a large enough crowd to provide the desired contribution. This motivation can be intrinsic or extrinsic. Behavioral studies on crowdsourcing highlight the significant role of intrinsic motivation in encouraging volunteer participation. Individuals are often driven by a sense of altruism and a desire to contribute to the safety and well-being of their communities. For instance, they may participate to enhance neighborhood safety while also benefiting from improved personal safety. Additionally, they gain valuable insights into local traffic conditions, ongoing incidents, or crime levels in their area. The primary motivations behind such participation are typically rooted in a sense of justice, the aspiration to help others by preventing further victimization, and the fulfillment of being part of a meaningful cause. 

Reshaping Narratives: How UGC Empowers Communities and Enhances Safety 

Beyond its remarkable timeliness mentioned above, UGC reshapes the power of narrative. With UGC, residents can directly share their perspectives without relying on official narratives. This decentralized mode of information dissemination empowers ordinary individuals to become “community journalists,” enhancing transparency and democratizing access to information. 
UGC also enables a sense of empowerment, allowing ordinary individuals to take an active role in shaping the safety of their communities. Unlike traditional systems that rely solely on institutional control, this participatory approach decentralizes power, granting citizens a voice and role in responding to crises. This sense of empowerment often strengthens community bonds and collective responsibility, as contributors realize the tangible impact of their participation. Moreover, during emergencies or crises, platforms like Citizen enable emotional support by facilitating connections among neighbors, reinforcing the social fabric of communities. The broad participation enabled by UGC also improves information coverage, especially for communities in “news deserts” or those experiencing “data voids.” For marginalized groups, Citizen provides an invaluable resource for accessing critical updates about local incidents. As one user noted, “For many underserved communities, knowing about crime is a necessity.” By involving these groups in information creation and dissemination, UGC fills gaps left by traditional media and ensures that no community is left uninformed. 

Challenges of UGC in Citizen: Risks and Controversies

Information Disorder: The Challenge of Quality Control 

Although there are many potential benefits of UGC, the quality control of crowd contributions stands out as one of the most significant challenges. Crowds can create the information contents but at the same time can facilitate information disorder: misinformation, disinformation and mal-information. Crowd created information is a dominant element in what is sometimes called the post-truth era. A small piece of misleading information can constitute significant challenges to the information sharing group or society. This misinformation can reshape in various ways how information-driven communities make sense of their world. Unchecked misinformation on Citizen can erode trust, fuel unnecessary panic, or distort public perceptions of safety.

The Power of the Narrative: Liberation or Control?  

Nicol Turner Lee , director of the Center for Technology Innovation at the Brookings Institution says:

"Whoever is in control of that information, or whoever controls that app or that platform, they control the narrative"

On Citizen, the coexistence of the empowerment of narrative rights and the centralization of information control rights not only brings the liberating power of decentralization, but also the hidden danger of centralization leading to bias and conflict. 

Hamid Khan, founder of the Stop LAPD Spying Coalition, an anti-police-surveillance group, sees Citizen as part of a “culture of deputization and vigilantism” built on the “see something, say something” ethos of neighborhood watch, now “taking a more technological sort of spin.” He says tools like Citizen, driven by the biases that are often unavoidable in UGC, “are becoming a license to racially profile and go after some of the most vulnerable community members - particularly the unhoused - and to criminalize them.” Khan sees a direct line from 19th-century militias and slave-catching agencies to modern-day policing, all of which serve to create a “stalker state” in which “white supremacy is maintained,” race and poverty are policed, “land is secured and preserved,” and “undesirables” are removed from society. At the same time, UGC may reinforce the image of certain areas as “dangerous” or “criminal,” leading to geographic discrimination. This phenomenon exacerbates the divide between communities, stigmatizes entire communities, and places a large group of people under additional scrutiny. 

Turning Neighbors into Informants: The Risk of Over-Surveillance 

The UGC function encourages people to go towards crime scenes so that they can film them, rather than stay away from them. Safety experts and police departments alike have given warnings about the danger of this. Jim Thatcher, an urban studies professor at the University of Washington, Tacoma, points out: “If you give people this power to draw attention based around a dangerous event, then they are actually encouraged to seek out, or at worst manufacture, these dangerous events.” Additionally, UGC risks normalizing hyper-surveillance in residential areas. As Bridget Bridges, a doctoral student at the University of Pennsylvania, notes, “Citizen turns neighbors into informants, encouraging them to fear and report ‘suspicious’ neighbors.” This culture of suspicion can erode trust within communities and transform monitoring into a harmful hobby. For some users, the act of reporting itself becomes a form of entertainment, exacerbating tensions and increasing the likelihood of misreporting.

Recommendations

Strengthen Content Moderation with Cross-Verification 
Citizen should implement a robust moderation framework that combines AI tools, human oversight, and cross-verification mechanisms. AI can swiftly filter out potentially harmful or inaccurate content, while human moderators provide context-sensitive judgment. Additionally, cross-verification processes—such as comparing multiple user reports, corroborating information with reliable third-party sources, or using location-specific data—can ensure greater accuracy. This layered approach minimizes misinformation while maintaining the platform’s timeliness. 

Educate Users on Responsible Reporting 
Citizen should actively educate its user base about responsible and ethical reporting practices. This can include providing short tutorials, clear guidelines, and interactive training modules on identifying biases, ensuring accuracy, and prioritizing safety over sensationalism. Citizen could also implement a reward system to incentivize users for submitting verified and non-biased reports, fostering a culture of responsible participation. 

Limit Risks in Live Reporting 
To address the dangers of live streaming near active crime scenes, Citizen should encourage users to prioritize their safety by reporting incidents from a secure distance. The platform could implement geofencing tools to restrict live streaming in hazardous zones and provide safety prompts or notifications to remind users of potential risks. Educating users about safe practices and the importance of avoiding danger while reporting incidents would further promote responsible and secure engagement.
